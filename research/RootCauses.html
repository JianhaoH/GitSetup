Root Cause #1:

Stereotyping of PTSD make it hard for victims to seek medical attention early on and leads to victims suffering a lifetime with symptoms of PTSD worsening.

Root Cause #2:

Misdiagnosis and underdiagonsis of PTSD patients lead to less treatment , which leads to PTSD symptoms getting progressively worse and victims using substances to cope with their trauma.


Evidence #1
  • " With fewer than 30% of sexual assault survivors ever seeking services for the psychological sequelae of trauma and with the lifetime prevalence of PTSD ranging as high as 50% to 80% for this group , a need exists to identify women suffering from PTSD and to assist them to seek out a beneficial treatment."
Evidence #2
  • "Most participants reported avoiding treatment early on to circumvent a label of mental illness. Participants initially reported experiencing some degree of self-stigma; however, following engagement in treatment they predominantly resisted these stereotypes.
Evidence #1
  • "Beyond formal clinical interventions, this study also highlighted the importance of optimizing the likelihood of maltreated children having access to positive stable supports, as this reduced risk for PTSD in children."
Evidence #2
  • “In primary care (PC) settings, patients with post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), are often not diagnosed; 2% to 11% with PTSD actually have the diagnosis noted in the medical record. In addition, less than half of these patients with PTSD, or even fewer, actually receive treatment for PTSD”"

Explanation #1
  • Very few SA survivors seek medical attention for their trauma, which leads to them living with them their entire life and never confronting it.
Explanation #2
  • Due to public stigmatization and stereotypes of PTSD, some avoid treatment in the early stages of PTSD to avoid labels of mental illness.
Explanation #1
  • PTSD in children and foster/orphaned children in particular are often neglected as they don't have the access to the resources necessary.
Explanation #2
  • PTSD is often underdiagonsed and can sometimes be misdiagonsed with other mental illnesses as PTSD shares many of the symptoms of depression.

Implications #1
  • Those who experience a sudden traumatic event are often unwilling to seek medical help. Destigmatization of mental disorders may help victims of PTSD feel more comfortable seeking help.
Implications #2
  • Access to medical attention for PTSD is also critical towards recovery, children in foster homes or orphanages often are never treated for their PTSD. Diagonsis accuracy should also be increased and treatment options made more avalible.
